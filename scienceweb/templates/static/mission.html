Who we are

Peerland is the place where researchers meet editors and vice versa. It's a site where:

  1. Reviewers from all disciplines have the opportunity to become professionals of the peer review process and have their performance evaluated and rewarded.
  2. Editors find the best and most appropriate reviewers for their journals' articles in two minutes.
  3. Organizations, including laboratories, departments and nonprofit organizations can present their respective projects to receive donations from Peerland's users.

Objetives

Our objectives are to:

  1. Support the advancement of Scientific Research by
    • redirecting funds from the peer review work to research projects, laboratories, departments, etc.
    • finding new income opportunities for researchers that perform excellent peer review work at Peerland.
    • helping other nonprofit organizations recruit funds for their scientific projects
  2. Help editors
    • find and choose high quality reviewers (in less than two minutes) using our search engine.
    • provide a high quality review for a manuscript in less than two weeks, saving time and costs in the editorial process.
  3. Support nonprofit organizations by direct donations from Peerland platform's users. We'll be a channel for scientists/users to support Science (or other philanthropic organizations) with one click of a button.

Our Mission

Peerland is a project within its mother nonprofit organization SACSIS, the Society for the Improvement of Science, thus our mission is aligned with SACSIS on the promotion of scientific research and technological innovation.

We have focused on a particular problem in the scientific community within the publishing sector, which is nowadays a highly debated field. New initiatives are trying to reinvent the publishing and peer review system, while others are simply trying to find a different niche to improve their competitiveness.

Peerland is focused on the peer review system and its inherent characteristic that has been a taboo for decades: it is an unpaid job. The reason why it is an unpaid work remains unclear, but the main reason points to the possible corruption of the reviewer by being compensated for their work by an economic retribution. We believe this presumption of guilt is antiquated and lacks any scientific evidence.

A recent survey, The Peer Review Survey 2009, estimated that more than 40% of reviewers would feel more likely to review if they were economically compensated. Our own survey indicates that most (over 70%) of reviewers would like to be economically compensated. We also believe that compensating reviewers would increase not only their likelihood of accepting a manuscript for review, but also positively affecting the quality and timing of the review. In order to measure reviewer quality and commitment we also introduced the Reviewer Quality coefficient RQ, estimated mostly by the evaluation performed by the editors of journals after the review process. We are also working to include other reviewers and authors to collaborate in the evaluation of the RQ through the Journal Club.

Our aims in the short term are:

  1. Create the figure of the professional reviewer.
  2. Increase the quality while decreasing the time and cost of the peer review process
  3. Provide the option to reviewers of donating their peer review income to scientific projects and nonprofit organizations in need of funding

What are our future projects at Peerland?

As a nonprofit organization focused on supporting science, medical and technological projects, Peerland would make use of the profit generated through our platform to invest in different projects, depending on the funds collected and on the market needs. Some of the proposed projects might include the following:

  1. Adopt a PhD/Postdoc (give economical support to students to pursue their PhD thesis and/or postdoctoral fellow).
  2. Funding scientific, medical or technological projects or other different disciplines.
  3. Funding translational research.
  4. Supporting scientific and technological spin-off companies.

A non Profit

A nonprofit organization

Peerland is part of the Society for the Improvement of Science (SACSIS), a nonprofit scientific organization committed to reinvesting the profits into Science through different programmes: research fellowships, project grants, translational science programs, etc. SACSIS was founded to promote scientific research and technological innovation at the highest levels. Amongst others, SACSIS's general purpose is to prevent the editorial bias that is affecting the negative results of main scientific research. The All Results Journals was created as a four-set of total open access journals, widely supported by volunteers worldwide, in order to give negative results the importance they deserve and combat the existing publication bias in drug chemistry, biochemistry and biomedical research. The journals have a Scientific Advisory Board composed of more than 100 international researchers coming from many diverse areas, such as Nanotechnology, Molecular Biology, Cell Imaging, Bionanotechnology and Organic Chemistry, among others.

SACSIS is also managing other diverse programs like a) a specific educational program for young Spanish researchers ""Time management for scientists"" and b) promoting the access to European funding to Spanish (and Spanish speakers) researchers through a specific educational program and website.

Performance Evaluation (RQ)

Performance evaluation

We have created a method to analyse your performance in Peerland, the Reviewer Quality (RQ) coefficient (patent pending), to acknowledge your work as a peer reviewer. You can build your reputation as a reviewer by reviewing manuscripts from journals or by participating in a Journal Club.

Based on Peerland guidelines to conduct a solid peer review, we have also created a guide to evaluate the quality of the revision. This evaluation work is mostly carried out by the editor once the revision process has finished, except for the Journal Club, where the evaluation can be taken by both authors and reviewers.

Once you have registered in our platform and you have updated your publications and h-index, your will obtain a provisional RQ score calculated using our algorithm. This provisional score will position you within two categories based on your experience as a researcher: Novice (RQ<30) or Senior (RQ=30-60).

Once you begin reviewing through our platform, each evaluation will be integrated in our algorithm and your RQ will be re-calculated. After your third revision you will obtain your actual RQ. Once a reviewer gets an RQ over 60 points he/she will become a Seasoned/Sensei Reviewer.

This distinction can be used to determine the price an editor would need to pay for each reviewer per revision:

  • Novice - 30 euros
  • Senior - 50 euros
  • Seasoned - 100 euros
